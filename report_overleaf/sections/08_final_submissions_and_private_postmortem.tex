\subsection{Final-Evaluation Slot Decision}
At selection time (before private release), I used my two final-evaluation slots on \texttt{expanding\_all} and \texttt{robust\_anchor}. \texttt{zero} and the macro-specialist candidate with $\lambda=0.1$ were \textbf{not} selected for final evaluation. After private release, the postmortem showed that hard-zero tail behavior (as in \texttt{zero}) generalized better than both selected tail priors (\texttt{expanding\_all}, \texttt{robust\_anchor}) on the checked hidden split.

\subsection{Why local-CV ranking reversed on private LB}
Local cutoff-239 simulation favored non-zero tail priors over \texttt{zero} by small but CI-supported margins (e.g., \texttt{zero} $-$ \texttt{macro\_lam0p1\_global} mean diff $\approx +1.3157\times10^{-4}$, 95\% CI $[4.533\times10^{-5}, 2.2138\times10^{-4}]$). However, private outcomes reversed this ordering:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{zero} (hard-zero tail behavior): \texttt{0.0139}
  \item \texttt{expanding\_all}: \texttt{0.0152}
  \item \texttt{robust\_anchor}: \texttt{0.0153}
\end{itemize}
This indicates hidden-tail regime shift: the private tail day behaved closer to zero-centered continuation than to the historical average patterns that helped in local simulation.

\subsection{Why \texttt{zero} beat selected tail models in private}
Using \texttt{report\_overleaf/proofs/proof\_04\_tail\_submission\_behavior.py}, I verified:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{zero} uses hard-zero in all unknown windows.
  \item \texttt{expanding\_all} injects dynamic non-zero minute-mean values.
  \item \texttt{robust\_anchor} private score (\texttt{0.0153}) was also worse than \texttt{zero} (\texttt{0.0139}).
\end{itemize}

Therefore, on this private hidden split, the unknown tail behaved closer to zero-centered dynamics than the selected non-zero tail priors.

\subsection{Model diversity check}
I also computed pairwise correlation/MAE in unknown windows with:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{report\_overleaf/proofs/proof\_06\_submission\_correlation.py}
\end{itemize}
This showed \texttt{robust\_anchor} and macro-family variants were highly correlated in tail windows, while zero-style fallback was structurally different in short-tail behavior.

\subsection{Main lesson from postmortem}
Near-zero public scores can hide meaningful private differences. In this competition, tiny assumptions on the final unknown 240-minute horizon were enough to change rank.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Public leaderboard rounding to \texttt{0.0000} hides meaningful decimal-level differences.
  \item Local boundary simulation is a proxy, not an exact private-LB emulator.
  \item Distribution shift in the hidden tail day can reverse local-CV ranking.
  \item Highly correlated submission pairs reduce effective diversification.
\end{itemize}
